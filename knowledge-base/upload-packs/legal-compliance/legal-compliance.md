Hello felllow Uncaged!  This post is a follow-up to a post that I made last week about offering paid wellness visits that are NOT considered PT.  I met with my lawyer today, and she told me the following facts that I thought you all might find useful:

1) While it is possible (of course) to charge for this service, whether it is considered "legally physical therapy" or not is up to the law where the patient is present when receiving the service.  For example, If the patient is in Ontario when you performed the service (in person or over phone/video) you would be subject to laws in Ontario.  It is nearly impossible to know how each jurisdiction would behave in the event of a law violation or complaint EVERYWHERE in the world.  

2)  The typical course of action of a governing board such as a state PT board to a practic act violation would be a cease and desist.  If you are sent a cease and desist, you can try to present evidence that you are NOT, in fact, violating the practice act to see if you can convince them to let you continue, or you could simply cease and desist and let it go.  They would only have you cease and desist in THEIR jurisdiction.  For example if the California state board felt that you were performing PT without a license there, they would send you a letter demanding you cease and desist what you are doing IN California.  The chances of legal repercussions or losing your license is low.

3)  You need to advertise what you are doing clearly and specify how it is NOT physical therapy.  The best method to do this is to define Physical therapy as precisely as possible (I.e. performed by a licensed physical therapist, consists of an evaluation, PT diagnoses, customized goals and treatment), and then point out that your wellness program is NOT that (i.e. can be performed by a person who is NOT a licensed physical therapist, does NOT include an evaluation, PT diagnosis, or customized goals and treatment).

4) My lawyer suggested having a contract for the client to sign where they agree to terms, including that they have been informed that this is not PT and will not include anything that would be considered PT or would take the place of medical treatment.  I'm sure that's not all the contract will cover, but she is writing up something formal for me.

5)  Medical liability insurance may not cover a consultation like service.  You need to check with your liability insurance company about whether they would cover it, and if not you need to get general business liability insurance.

6)  Keep records of visits, but those records should not look like a medical record or a Pt evaluative or treatment note in any way.

7)  Treatment or advice cannot be custom unless you are referring to things other providers have already diagnosed them with/advised them of.  Example:  if a neurologist has diagnosed someone with Parkinson's, you can provide them with a general handout from the Parkinson's foundation and go over it with them.  Example 2:  If a doctor had ordered PT for this person recently, but the patient refused to go due to the time commitment as the clinic is far away, you could encourage them to go to PT and help them find a clinic that is nearer to them.

8.  USE YOUR PT KNOWLEDGE to know what is considered NOT PT!  You are literally the expert witness they might call to see if someone who is claiming not to practice PT is practicing PT!  You know the definition because you are the expert.  So don't do anything that is considered PT!
